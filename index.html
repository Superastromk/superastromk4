What is Syntax?

- Syntax (in a linguistic sense) is the way words or phrases go together in a language.

What are the Parts of Syntax?

The parts of syntax are:

  • Phrases (Noun, Adjective, Adverb, Verb, Prepositional)
  • Determiners
  • Clauses (relative, adverbial, coordinate)
  • Word Order

    Syntax in Other Fields

  • Math : Syntax is the part of mathematics that deals with symbols, and rules for manipulating them.
  • Coding/Programming : It refers to the rules and regulations for writing any statement in a programming language (like C / C++). ​

    What is Word Order?

    Word order, as it says in the name, is the order in which words go in a language.

    The word order for English, for example, is SVO, which means that the order of an English phrase will have a basic structure of subject, object, then verb.

    Word order in other languages include:

  • Japanese: SOV
  • Arabic: SVO and VSO
  • Russian: OVS

    What are constituents?

    A constituent is a word or group of words which function as a single unit, and pass at least one of the three constituency tests

    The constituency tests are:

  • Replacement- phrase where you can replace it with the same part of speech and get a sentence with the same meaning.
    E.g., The students saw a movie after class. --> The students saw it after class.
  • Movement- Some constituents can be moved to somewhere else in the sentence without changing the sentence’s meaning or its grammaticality.
    E.g., Jack bought a scarf at that strange shop. --> At that strange shop, Jack bought a scarf.
  • Cleft- Where you take two parts of a sentence and divide them from each other.
    E.g., The students saw a movie after class. --> It was a movie that the students saw after class.​

    The kinds of phrases

    What is a phrase?

    A phrase is a word or group of words functioning as a unit.

    What are the parts of a phrase?

    Phrases consist of the head and it's complements. The head is what the phrase is named after. For example, if the head is "armadillo", then it is a noun phrase. The complement is necessary to convey and complete the information that is trying to be expressed with the phrase.

    Noun Phrases

    A noun phrase, or nominal (phrase), is a phrase that has a noun or pronoun as its head or performs the same grammatical function as a noun.


    Examples:
    The spotted puppy was up for adoption.​
  • The spotted puppy is the noun phrase, because it can be replaced with any noun(replacement test)​
    Has anyone seen my striped cat?​
  • This could become --> Everyone has seen my striped cat.​

    Verb Phrases

    The part of a sentence containing the verb and any direct or indirect object, but not the subject.


    Examples:
    She was walking quickly to her house​
  • Was walking quickly can be replaced by running(verb). Which is an example of replacement test. ​
    He ate the food. ​
  • Ate can be moved --> The food he ate.​

    Adverb Phrases

    A group of words that acts like an adverb—i.e., it modifies a verb, adjective, adverb, or even a whole clause.
    Like adverbs, adverbial phrases can be used to describe how, where, when, and why.


    Examples:
    She ate her breakfast very quickly.​
  • Very quickly could be moved to the front --> Very quickly, she ate her breakfast. ​

    Prepositional phrases

    A prepositional phrase is a phrase which has it's head as a preposition. In other words, a prepositional phrase is a group of words consisting of a preposition, its object, and any words that modify the object.


    Examples:
    She hid under the table.
  • "under the table" is the prepositional phrase in this case, and can be moved to become "Under the table, she hid."

    Adjective Phrases

    An adjective phrase, or adjectival phrase, is a group of words that include an adjective that modifies (changes) a noun or pronoun.


    Examples:
    Someone smarter than me has to explain this.
  • In this case, "smarter than me" is the adjective phrase. This can be moved to "To explain this, someone has to be smarter than me."

    Syntax Trees

    A syntax tree is a representation of the structure of a single sentence – of the way that the individual words are grouped into phrases and eventually form the full sentence.
